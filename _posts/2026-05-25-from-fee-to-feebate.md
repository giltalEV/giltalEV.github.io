Paying for the roads we drive on is one of the more legitimate things the federal government asks of us. The federal gasoline excise tax of 18.4 cents per gallon funds the Highway Trust Fund, which pays for highways, bridges, and transit. The principle is sound: people who use the roads should pay to maintain them, in rough proportion to how much they use them.

The problem is that the federal gas tax has been frozen at 18.4 cents per gallon since 1993. It has not been raised, it has not been indexed to inflation, and it has lost more than half of its real purchasing power over three decades. At the same time, every car on the road has become more efficient. The average new vehicle sold in the United States got 20.9 MPG in 1993, according to EPA Trends Report data. It got 27.2 MPG today. The side effect is that every gas-vehicle driver, including the F-150 driver and the minivan driver and the commuter sedan driver, contributes fewer gallons of fuel per mile, and therefore fewer cents of federal gas tax per mile, than the same driver would have contributed in 1993. Add in the growing share of vehicles that are partially or fully electrified, and the Highway Trust Fund's revenue base is eroding from multiple directions at once. Something has to change.

The BUILD America 250 Act, the House surface transportation reauthorization introduced on May 17 and marked up on May 21, 2026, takes a first swing at that problem. Section 1129 would impose an annual federal registration fee of $130 on every battery electric vehicle and $35 on every plug-in hybrid, collected by state DMVs starting October 1, 2026. The fee escalates by $5 every two years until it reaches $150 for BEVs and $50 for PHEVs, then terminates in 2036. The framing is straightforward: EV drivers do not pay federal gas tax, so they should pay something equivalent.

It is worth pausing on what "first swing at the problem" actually means here. The problem, as just described, is that the gas tax has lost half its real value and that the entire light-duty fleet has become more efficient. The solution Congress reached for picks one segment of one small slice of that problem: the cleanest vehicles. Not the gas tax rate itself, which has been frozen for thirty-three years. Not the broader fleet, which is increasingly efficient and therefore increasingly under-paying relative to a 1993 baseline. Not commercial trucking, which does the most road damage per mile and whose diesel tax has also not been indexed. Just BEVs and PHEVs, which together account for about 10 percent of new vehicle sales. The Highway Trust Fund will not be saved by collecting $130 per year from a couple of million EV drivers. The fee solves a symbolic problem about the cleanest vehicles "not paying their share," not the actual revenue problem.

That does not mean the fee is unreasonable in principle. The underlying argument that EV drivers should contribute to road funding is legitimate. But the way the fee is structured deserves a closer look, because the structure matters more than the headline number. The same $130 can be a reasonable contribution from one household and an inequitable burden on another, depending on how much they drive, what they would have driven instead, and how old their vehicle is. The arithmetic of "fair share" is not as simple as $130 implies.

## The federal gas tax as a prepaid bundle

A flat annual fee on an EV is not really a road-use charge. It is a prepayment. The question is: how much gasoline tax is the federal government claiming the EV driver would otherwise have paid?

The arithmetic is direct. At 18.4 cents per gallon, $130 buys exactly 706.5 gallons of federal gasoline excise tax. The $130 fee is the gas tax equivalent of burning 706.5 gallons of gasoline per year, every year, regardless of whether the EV driver actually drives, what they drive, or how efficient their alternative would have been.

Spread that 706.5 gallons across the typical annual mileage of a new vehicle in the United States, which is approximately 13,500 miles per year for vehicles in their first five years of life according to FHWA data, and the implicit comparison vehicle is one that gets 19.1 miles per gallon. The actual average new gas vehicle sold in the United States in model year 2024 gets 25.5 MPG, according to the EPA Automotive Trends Report. Including BEVs and PHEVs in the fleet average pulls the figure up to 27.2 MPG. So the federal government, in setting the $130 fee, has implicitly assumed EV drivers would otherwise be driving a vehicle below every passenger sedan and most light SUVs sold today. And the 19.1 MPG figure understates the asymmetry: the 18.4 cent gas tax it is calibrated against has itself lost half its real value since 1993, so had the tax been indexed to inflation, the same $130 would imply only about 351 gallons, or a 38 MPG comparison vehicle.

That is the comparison baked into Section 1129. Not the average new gas car, not even a typical SUV, but a 19 MPG vehicle. A full-size V-8 pickup.

## Who comes out ahead at 19.1 MPG and 13,500 miles

The break-even is precise. A federal EV fee of $130 at 13,500 miles per year equals the federal gas tax paid by a gas vehicle that gets 19.1 MPG combined. For perspective, the Ford F-150 with the 5.0-liter V-8 engine gets 19 MPG combined, according to EPA fueleconomy.gov ratings for the 2025 model year. At 13,500 miles per year, that F-150 burns 710 gallons of gasoline and pays $130.74 in federal gas tax, almost exactly the $130 BEV fee. The policy is calibrated such that an EV driver pays roughly the same as a V-8 pickup driver.

There are two ways to come out ahead under Section 1129. The first is to drive a lot, because the flat fee dilutes per-mile with every additional mile. At 25,000 miles per year, the $130 fee works out to a 35 MPG comparison, better than most compact sedans. The second is to have been driving an unusually inefficient vehicle: going from a 15 MPG V-8 SUV to a Tesla Model Y is a net win for the driver because the gas tax avoided exceeds $130. The fee is generous to high-mileage commuters and to drivers replacing the least efficient vehicles in the fleet.

## Who loses

Everyone else.

A driver replacing an average new gas car loses. The US new-vehicle average is 27.2 MPG, and excluding BEVs and PHEVs from the calculation it is 25.5 MPG. At 13,500 miles per year, the average new gas car burns about 530 gallons and pays roughly $97 in federal gas tax. The same driver in a Tesla Model 3 or Kia EV6 pays $130 under Section 1129, a 34 percent increase in federal road-use tax for the act of buying a cleaner car.

A driver replacing an efficient hybrid loses badly. The 2025 Toyota Prius LE gets 57 MPG combined and pays $44 in federal gas tax at 13,500 miles per year. A driver going from a Prius to a Tesla Model 3 or any other BEV pays $130, an increase of nearly 200 percent.

The Prius Prime plug-in hybrid case is illustrative of the PHEV problem. A Prius Prime driver who plugs in every night and uses the 44 miles of EV range to cover most daily driving might use the gas engine only on weekends and longer trips. The vehicle still pays $35 under Section 1129 even though the driver buys almost no gasoline. The $35 fee divided by 18.4 cents per gallon equals 190 gallons of equivalent gas tax. At 13,500 miles per year, that is a 71 MPG comparison vehicle, exceeding any production passenger vehicle sold today.

## The affordability problem

Affordability is the right word for what Section 1129 does to the wrong households.

A flat $130 fee applies the same charge to every BEV owner regardless of how much they drive, what their household income is, or how old their vehicle is. On a per-mile basis, a BEV driver who logs 25,000 miles per year pays 0.5 cents per mile in federal road-use tax. A BEV driver who logs 5,000 miles per year pays 2.6 cents per mile, more than five times as much. The fee penalizes low driving and rewards high driving. That is the opposite of how an affordability-sensitive fee structure would be designed.

Who drives less? Retirees on fixed incomes. Low-income households whose vehicle covers essential trips rather than long commutes. Households where a BEV serves as a second car for short trips. These are precisely the populations that affordability-focused EV policy has spent the last decade trying to bring into electric vehicle ownership.

The used-EV affordability problem is the sharpest version of this. New BEVs are expensive: Kelley Blue Book put the average transaction price for a new BEV in late 2025 at $58,000 to $59,000, well out of reach for most households below median income. The affordability case for EV adoption rests on the used market, where a used Chevy Bolt costs $10,000 to $14,000 and a used Nissan Leaf $6,000 to $9,000. Those vehicles are also driven less. FHWA National Household Travel Survey data show vehicles aged one to five years average over 12,000 miles per year, while vehicles over nine years old average about 7,800 miles per year. Lower purchase price, lower annual mileage, lower-income owners. Section 1129 charges them all the same $130.

On a per-mile basis, the used-BEV household pays 1.7 cents per mile in federal road-use tax. The new-BEV household pays 1.0 cent per mile. The lower-income, lower-mileage household pays 70 percent more per mile than the higher-income, higher-mileage household for the same federal benefit.

Stack the federal $130 fee on top of state EV fees, which exist or are proposed in many states and range from $50 to over $200 per year, and the affordability problem compounds. A used-BEV household in California pays the $121 state ZEV road improvement fee plus the $130 federal fee, totaling $251 per year, on a vehicle that may be worth $8,000 to $12,000. A comparable 10-year-old gas car at 25 MPG and 7,800 miles per year burns 312 gallons and pays $57 in federal gas tax, so the used-BEV household pays over twice as much federal road-use tax as a comparable gas household. The policy as drafted asks the lowest-income, lowest-mileage, oldest-vehicle EV households to subsidize the federal Highway Trust Fund at rates that no gas-vehicle driver in their situation would pay.

This is the affordability problem. Section 1129 doesn't break the EV market for affluent new-car buyers. It breaks the affordability case for everyone else.

## Where this could go: states as the balancing layer

A flawed federal policy is not the end of the story. The federal road-use fee, even badly designed, can set in motion a more productive conversation at the state level. Three categories of state-level response are worth serious analysis, and concrete precedents already exist for each.

The first is affordability-targeted relief for used-EV households. If the federal fee falls hardest on the second and third owners of older BEVs, a state-level offsetting credit, tied to vehicle age and household income, can restore the affordability case for used EV ownership. Several states already operate income-tested purchase incentives for used EVs. Extending that logic to the registration phase would neutralize the federal fee for the households who need the protection.

The second is a per-mile component on road pricing. The fundamental problem with Section 1129 is that it is flat. A road-use charge that combines a small base fee with a per-mile component would tax road use the way the gas tax was supposed to, in proportion to actual use. Effective July 1, 2025, the Hawaii Road Usage Charge program (HiRUC) gives EV owners the choice of paying a flat $50 annual fee or $8 per 1,000 miles driven, capped at $50 per year, with mileage captured at annual vehicle inspections. Beginning in 2028, the per-mile structure becomes the only option for EVs, and by 2033 the program is expected to extend to all light-duty vehicles. Other states running road-charge pilots can adopt similar structures. A state per-mile fee combined with the federal $130 flat fee at least introduces a use-proportional element into the total bill.

The third is a feebate. A feebate charges a fee on inefficient vehicles at purchase and uses the revenue to rebate buyers of efficient vehicles. The mechanism is well-understood and has been implemented in France, the Netherlands, and Norway. Hawaii's HB2688 in the 2024 session proposed exactly this: a clean vehicle special fund financed by a graduated tax on the purchase of gasoline-powered passenger cars and pickup trucks based on their fuel efficiency, with revenues used to rebate buyers of cleaner vehicles. The Section 1129 federal fee is a partial step toward feebate logic, in the wrong direction: it surcharges the cleanest vehicles. A state feebate that scales by efficiency, with the rebate side preserving the buyer incentive for ZEVs and high-MPG hybrids, would offset the federal surcharge and re-establish the price signal that the federal policy weakens.

These three mechanisms — affordability relief, per-mile pricing, and feebates — can convert a poorly designed federal policy into a more coherent state framework. None of them require federal action. Some states, Hawaii most concretely, are already doing the work.

## What to watch

Section 1129 is not a disaster. The federal Highway Trust Fund needs revenue, EV drivers do use the roads, and the political math that produced this fee is unlikely to be relitigated this year. The interesting question is what comes next. A bad policy at the federal level can prompt a better one at the state level. If states respond to Section 1129 by building affordability-sensitive, use-proportional, signal-preserving alternatives through some combination of used-EV relief, per-mile road charges, and feebates, the chain reaction set off by a flawed federal fee could end somewhere more productive than where it started.

That outcome is not automatic. It requires the state policy community to treat Section 1129 not as a problem to be denounced but as an opening to be used. The fee will land on EV drivers either way. Whether it lands as a flat regressive surcharge or as the entry point to a more rational road-use framework depends on what states choose to do with it.
