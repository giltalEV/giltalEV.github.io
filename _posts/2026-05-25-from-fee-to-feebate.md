Congress is debating Section 1129 of the budget reconciliation bill, which would impose a fee on purchases of electric vehicles. The stated goal is fiscal: recoup some of the revenue lost when the Inflation Reduction Act's Section 30D tax credit is eliminated. The effect, in most analyses, would be to slow ZEV adoption at exactly the moment the market needs to cross from early-majority to mainstream.

There is a better design available, and it does not require abandoning the fiscal constraint.

## What a Feebate Is

A feebate is a fee-and-rebate structure applied within the same product category. High-emitting vehicles pay a fee at purchase; low-emitting vehicles receive a rebate. The fee revenue funds the rebates. At the right design point, the system is roughly revenue neutral for the government. The price signal discourages high-emission choices and rewards low-emission ones, without the government making a net transfer either to industry or away from it.

California has operated a version of this through the Clean Vehicle Rebate Project and, more directly, through the Advanced Clean Cars program's compliance structure. France has run an explicit feebate (the *bonus-malus*) since 2008. The empirical literature on feebates is now substantial enough to say with confidence that they work: they shift sales mix, they are less regressive than fuel taxes on a per-vehicle basis, and they can be designed to hit any desired revenue target including zero.

## How Section 1129 Could Be Redesigned

The current draft imposes a flat fee on EVs. The redesign is straightforward in principle:

**Step 1.** Define a carbon intensity threshold within the new-vehicle market — the point at which a vehicle neither pays nor receives. This is the "pivot point."

**Step 2.** Vehicles above the threshold (higher tailpipe emissions) pay a fee proportional to how far above the threshold they fall.

**Step 3.** Vehicles below the threshold (lower tailpipe or zero tailpipe emissions) receive a rebate proportional to how far below the threshold they fall.

**Step 4.** Calibrate the fee and rebate schedules so that projected revenues from fees approximately equal projected rebate outlays — revenue neutrality.

The pivot point and the steepness of the fee/rebate slope are the two policy dials. A steep slope accelerates the transition faster but creates larger transfers between vehicle classes. A shallow slope is more modest. Either can be made revenue neutral.

## Why This Matters More Than the Tax Credit Debate

The IRA's Section 30D credit was a subsidy paid by the federal government to EV buyers. When the credit goes away, the question is what, if anything, replaces the price signal. A flat fee on EVs (the current Section 1129 draft) removes the carrot and adds a stick. A feebate removes the carrot but replaces it with a different carrot — one funded by fees on the alternatives.

From a market-efficiency standpoint, a feebate is actually preferable to a tax credit in several respects. A tax credit benefits buyers who would have purchased an EV anyway; a feebate concentrates incentives at the margin of decision where they actually change behavior. A tax credit draws on general revenue; a feebate is self-funding within the sector.

The political path is harder. Automakers making heavy trucks and SUVs will lobby against any fee structure that disadvantages their high-margin products. But the revenue-neutrality argument provides some insulation: the government is not taking money out of the industry, it is redistributing within it.

## What the Data Show About Demand Sensitivity

Our research group's surveys and registration data give us reasonable purchase-price elasticity estimates for the U.S. new-vehicle market in the EV segment. The short version: a $1,000 fee equivalent at the margin reduces EV share by roughly 1.5–2.5 percentage points in California markets and somewhat more in less-developed EV markets. A $3,000–4,000 fee — which is roughly what the current Section 1129 draft implies — would put meaningful downward pressure on sales at a moment when the industry is trying to break through to mainstream buyers who are more price-sensitive than the early adopters who carried the first decade of EV growth.

A revenue-neutral feebate calibrated to the same fee level on the high end would produce a net incentive for EV buyers, not a fee. The sales impact would flip sign.

## The Legislative Path

The cleanest approach is an amendment to Section 1129 that (a) retains the fee on high-emission vehicles, (b) creates a corresponding rebate for ZEVs and low-emission vehicles, and (c) directs Treasury to publish annual pivot-point and schedule calibrations to maintain revenue neutrality. None of this requires new administrative infrastructure; NHTSA's existing fuel economy data and EPA's emissions certification data are sufficient inputs.

The harder question is timing. Reconciliation moves fast. If the amendment path closes before this analysis reaches the relevant staff, the fallback is a subsequent standalone bill or a provision in the next appropriations vehicle. The feebate structure is not contingent on Section 1129 specifically — it can be enacted on its own.
