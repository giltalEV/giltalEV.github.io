The Q1 2026 EV sales numbers are in, and they confirm what those of us watching the underlying data have been saying for eighteen months: the deceleration in EV sales growth was not primarily a demand story. It was a supply-chain story, an incentive-expiration story, and — most importantly — a market-maturation story that was always going to play out this way at this moment.

## What the Numbers Show

National EV market share in Q1 2026 came in at approximately 7.8 percent of new vehicle sales, down from a peak of 9.1 percent in Q3 2025. That is a meaningful drop. The headlines called it a cliff. Our research group's read is more precise: it is a stair-step, the kind of pattern you see when early-adopter saturation meets a policy transition.

California — always the leading indicator — is at 23 percent EV share, down from 26 percent. The rest of the country is at roughly 5 percent. The gap between California and everywhere else is the real story here.

## Why "Buyers Ran Away" Is the Wrong Frame

The most common media narrative attributes the deceleration to consumer reluctance: range anxiety, charging infrastructure gaps, sticker price, political polarization of the EV brand. These factors are real. They are not, however, new. They were present during the growth phase too. If they are now causing a decline, something else changed.

What changed:

**The IRA Section 30D credit expired** for several popular models as manufacturers hit the 200,000-vehicle cap (under the old rules) or as new eligibility rules knocked out specific vehicle configurations. The average effective subsidy available to a new EV buyer dropped by roughly $2,500 between Q2 2025 and Q1 2026.

**Early-adopter saturation in mature markets.** California, Oregon, Washington, and the Northeast — the markets where EV awareness and infrastructure are strongest — have already moved a large fraction of the environmentally motivated, technology-enthusiastic buyer pool into EVs. The remaining ICE buyers in these markets skew toward price-sensitive, range-dependent, or politically skeptical profiles. These buyers are the next group to convert, not the current group, and they convert more slowly.

**Inventory normalization.** During 2023–2025, dealer inventory of popular EVs was constrained by production limits. As production caught up with demand, visible inventory accumulated on lots. Paradoxically, high visible inventory signals low demand to consumers even when it reflects improved supply. The "lots full of EVs" story became part of the media narrative in ways that reinforced hesitancy.

## What This Means for Policy

The distinction matters for policy design. If the slowdown is primarily behavioral — consumers choosing ICE because they prefer it — the policy response is demand-side: more education, better test-drive programs, expanded incentives. If the slowdown is structural — expiring incentives, saturation of the early-adopter pool, supply normalization — the policy response is different: redesigned incentives that reach the mainstream buyer (higher income caps, used-vehicle credits, charging infrastructure at MDUs), and patience.

The data strongly support the structural interpretation. Our Consumer Survey data shows that awareness, consideration rates, and purchase intent among non-EV households have *not* fallen. They have continued to rise slowly. What has fallen is the conversion rate at the final decision point, which is where price and financing sensitivity matter most.

This is an argument for feebate-style policy structures rather than flat subsidies: instruments that concentrate incentive dollars at the margin of decision rather than distributing them to buyers who would have purchased an EV anyway.

## The Used Market Is the Next Chapter

One signal we are watching closely: used EV prices have stabilized after a sharp decline in 2024. The lease-return wave that flooded the used market in 2023–2024 is subsiding. A reasonably priced used EV (under $25,000) with an established reliability record is a fundamentally different product from the $45,000 new EV that dominated early adoption. The used market may do more for mainstream adoption over the next three years than any federal incentive policy.
